Persistence over time

Under the canopy

Through our work a path appears as we uncover native plants from under tangled invasive plants of all sizes. A patch of multiflora rose bushes can be seen in the bottom right corner, yet to be removed. The red and orange adoloscent sassafrass trees in the center of the picture will someday become the new canopy. After every patch of invasive plants that we remove there are opportunities for native plants to thrive under the canopy similar to the sassafrass.




Pockets of sun

The edge of the work area. A dense wall of multiflora rose stands tall just beyond the recently uncovered blackberry patch in the bottom center of the picture. Dead ash trees have opened the canopy to established invasive plants providing them with ample sunlight and more energy to grow. Without intervention the invasive plants will dominate for years to come.




Sun all day

Planting opportunities abound. A dense region of ash trees died creating this open area with conditions drastically different from under the canopy. The invasive plant compost trellis can be seen as a dark patch weaving through the trees in the left center. The space was planted with a variety of native fruiting shrubs and trees. Seeds from native flowering plants were spread prolifically.




Logs on contour

Slowing down water mitigates erosion and keeps the hillside fertile. Log swales provide a great place to plant new native trees without the need for continued watering.




Access to apples

As the invasive plants are cleared from beneath productive trees they are placed in tactfully located piles. These piles return nutrients back to the soil as they decay naturally and in the mean time provide protected homes for wildlife.
